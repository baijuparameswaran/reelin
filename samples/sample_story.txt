The Lighthouse Keeper's Last Night

For forty years, Edith had kept the light burning on Mara's Point. The villagers
below called her the Widow of the Rock, though no one living remembered a husband.
Every night she climbed the ninety-seven steps, trimmed the wick, and watched the
black water for ships that needed her.

On the night the automation crew was due to arrive, a storm came first. The radio
crackled with a distress call: a fishing boat, the Saoirse, taking on water two
miles out, engine dead. The coast guard could not launch until dawn. By dawn the
crew of three would be gone.

Edith had a rowboat she had not touched in twenty years. Her hands were old. The
sea did not care. She stood at the top of the tower with the radio in one hand and
the lamp's glow at her back, and she made a choice no one would ever know she made.

She left the light burning, untended, and went down to the water. The villagers
would find the lighthouse dark only once in its history — that night — and never
understand why. But the three sailors of the Saoirse would walk into the harbor at
sunrise, soaked and alive, speaking of an old woman in a rowboat who appeared out
of the rain, tied their hands to her oars, and pulled them home.

By the time the automation crew reached the top of the tower, the lamp had gone
cold and Edith was asleep in her chair by the harbor, salt in her white hair. She
never kept the light again. She never had to. But for one night, the keeper had
been the rescue, not the warning — and that, she decided, was what the light had
always been for.
